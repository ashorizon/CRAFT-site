Overview

Closed-loop simulation exposes global context that ego-centric logs cannot fully capture.

Autoregressive traffic simulators learn from ego-centric driving logs, where surrounding context can be incomplete due to perception limits and occlusions. When these models are rolled out in globally observable simulation, incomplete context-action associations can lead to abnormal stops, unsafe interactions, and rule violations.

Contributions

From incomplete log imitation to complete-context preference alignment.

Local-to-global mismatch

Ego-centric logs can create incomplete context–action associations that degrade behavioral rationality in globally observable closed-loop rollouts.

Complete-context preference learning

Grouped what-if rollouts expose simulator-induced failures and provide token-level, intra-trajectory, and inter-rollout supervision for CPE.

Preference-guided decoding

CPE evaluates one-step lookahead scenes and recalibrates candidate probabilities toward globally coherent behavior before execution.

Method

CRAFT turns simulator-induced failures into preference guidance.

CRAFT first learns a Contextual Preference Evaluator from complete-context what-if rollouts, then uses it during inference to reweight candidate actions before they are executed.

Training

Learn complete-context preferences from what-if rollouts.

A frozen autoregressive simulator generates diverse rollouts from the same logged initial scene. These grouped futures expose simulator-induced failures, which are converted into dense preference supervision for CPE.

Inference

Calibrate candidate actions before execution.

At each simulation step, CPE scores one-step lookahead scenes induced by candidate actions and reweights the simulator distribution toward globally coherent behavior.

Main results

CRAFT improves closed-loop behavioral rationality.

Under all-agent behavioral metrics, CRAFT substantially reduces collisions, offroad events, and traffic-rule violations while maintaining competitive distributional realism. The mild increase in nearest-agent distance JSD is consistent with CRAFT favoring safer inter-agent spacing.

Test-time alignment analysis

CPE warns before failures emerge and reduces long-horizon error accumulation.

Dense preference scores reveal risky action distributions before explicit violations occur, allowing CRAFT to recalibrate the next-token distribution before the action is executed.

Robustness

Training scale, guidance strength, and candidate size jointly affect test-time alignment.

Increasing the preference-training data consistently improves behavioral metrics, indicating that CPE benefits from broader coverage of simulator-induced failure modes. At inference time, the guidance scale β controls the strength of calibration: a small β provides limited correction, while an overly large β over-calibrates the base distribution and degrades performance. Increasing the candidate pool from 16 to 32 improves guidance quality, but a larger pool introduces noisier candidates and reduces stability.
