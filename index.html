Abstract

Autoregressive traffic simulators trained on ego-centric driving logs can suffer from a local-to-global context mismatch when deployed in globally observable closed-loop environments. This mismatch may cause unrealistic behaviors such as abnormal stops, unsafe interactions, and rule violations.

We propose CRAFT, a Contextual Reference Alignment Framework for Traffic Simulation. CRAFT discovers context-induced failures through what-if rollouts, trains a Contextual Preference Evaluator, and uses it as a plug-in test-time guidance module to improve closed-loop realism, safety, and rule compliance without retraining the base simulator.

Overview

CRAFT targets the mismatch between partial observations in driving logs and complete context in closed-loop simulation.

Method Overview

CRAFT closes the gap between ego-centric local observations and globally observable closed-loop simulation through two complementary stages: contextual preference learning during training and plug-in test-time alignment during inference.

Stage 1

Training Strategy: Contextual Preference Learning

CRAFT keeps the base traffic simulator frozen and uses its closed-loop rollouts to discover simulator-induced failures. These failures are converted into preference supervision under complete scene context, enabling the Contextual Preference Evaluator (CPE) to learn which candidate behaviors are more rational, safe, and rule-compliant.

  1. What-if rollout: generate diverse closed-loop rollouts from logged initial scenes.
  2. Failure discovery: detect abnormal stops, collisions, off-road events, and traffic violations.
  3. Preference construction: compare better and worse rollouts under the same global scene context.
  4. CPE training: learn dense contextual preference scores for candidate behaviors.
Stage 2

Inference Strategy: Plug-in Test-Time Alignment

At test time, CPE evaluates candidate actions under one-step lookahead scenes and reweights the frozen simulator distribution. This provides contextual guidance without retraining or modifying the base traffic simulator.

  1. Extract Top-K candidate actions from the base simulator.
  2. Construct one-step lookahead scenes for each candidate.
  3. Use CPE to estimate contextual preference scores.
  4. Reweight base probabilities and sample the final action.

Robustness Analysis

Robustness to Training and Inference Configurations. The figure shows that CRAFT remains robust across both training and inference configurations. Increasing the amount of preference training data consistently improves behavioral metrics, suggesting that CPE benefits from broader coverage of simulator-induced failure modes. During test-time alignment, a moderate guidance scale β achieves the best trade-off: a small β provides limited correction, whereas an overly large β over-calibrates the base distribution and degrades performance. A similar trend appears for candidate size K: increasing K from 16 to 32 improves guidance quality, while a larger candidate pool introduces noisier actions and reduces rollout stability.

Key Findings

  • Better closed-loop safety: CRAFT reduces collision, off-road, and traffic violation rates.
  • General plug-in guidance: CRAFT improves both CAT-K and SMART without retraining the base simulator.
  • More rational rollouts: CRAFT mitigates abnormal stops and unsafe interactions.
  • Stable test-time alignment: CPE provides proactive risk signals and reduces error accumulation.
