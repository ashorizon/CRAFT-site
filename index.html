Overview

Ego-centric driving logs omit global context that becomes available in closed-loop simulation.

Autoregressive traffic simulators are trained on ego-centric logs, where perception limits and occlusions hide part of the context surrounding other agents. When the learned context–action associations are executed in a globally observable closed-loop scene, locally plausible actions can become contextually inconsistent, causing abnormal stopping, unsafe interactions, and rule violations.

Method

CRAFT learns from failures exposed in complete-context what-if rollouts, then corrects decoding before execution.

The frozen simulator first serves as a globally observable sandbox for self-supervised failure discovery. A Contextual Preference Evaluator (CPE) then converts these failures into dense behavioral preferences and applies them as test-time guidance.

Training

Learn complete-context preferences from grouped alternative futures.

Starting from the same logged initial scene, the frozen simulator generates multiple stochastic what-if rollouts. Human-aligned driving priors annotate safety, compliance, and kinematic failures at the agent–time level. CPE is trained with token-level supervision together with intra-trajectory and inter-rollout preference constraints.

Inference

Evaluate one-step lookahead scenes and recalibrate candidate probabilities.

At each decoding step, the base simulator proposes Top-K candidate actions. Each candidate is applied to construct a one-step lookahead scene, which CPE evaluates under the complete traffic context. The resulting preference scores reweight the original action probabilities before the next action is sampled.

Qualitative rollouts

CRAFT suppresses traffic-rule violations and abnormal stopping in closed-loop rollouts.

When CAT-K selects a locally likely but contextually unreasonable action, CRAFT uses complete-scene preference scores to suppress that action and preserve normal driving behavior.

Rule compliance

Traffic-signal compliance

CAT-K runs the red light, whereas CRAFT suppresses the violating action and reliably complies with the traffic signal.

Behavioral stability

Abnormal-stop mitigation

CAT-K stops without a valid cause and disrupts traffic flow, while CRAFT maintains smooth forward motion and flow stability.

Main results

CRAFT improves closed-loop behavioral rationality across CAT-K and SMART.

Under all-agent behavioral metrics, CRAFT substantially reduces collisions, offroad events, and traffic-rule violations while maintaining competitive distributional realism. The mild increase in nearest-agent distance JSD is consistent with CRAFT favoring safer inter-agent spacing.

Test-time alignment analysis

CPE warns before failures emerge and reduces long-horizon error accumulation.

Dense preference scores reveal risky action distributions before explicit violations occur, allowing CRAFT to recalibrate the next-token distribution before the action is executed.

Robustness

More preference data helps, while moderate guidance and candidate size work best.

Increasing the preference-training set consistently improves behavioral metrics. At inference, moderate guidance avoids both under-correction and over-calibration, while expanding the candidate pool from 16 to 32 improves guidance quality before noisier candidates begin to reduce stability.

Contributions

From incomplete log imitation to complete-context preference alignment.

Local-to-global mismatch

Ego-centric logs can create incomplete context–action associations that degrade behavioral rationality in globally observable closed-loop rollouts.

Complete-context preference learning

Grouped what-if rollouts expose simulator-induced failures and provide token-level, intra-trajectory, and inter-rollout supervision for CPE.

Preference-guided decoding

CPE evaluates one-step lookahead scenes and recalibrates candidate probabilities toward globally coherent behavior before execution.
